How to Read Tarot Card Combinations

A tarot spread is more than a collection of separate card meanings. The cards interact, support, challenge and change one another. Learning to connect them into one clear message is one of the most important steps in becoming a confident tarot reader.

✦

What Is a Tarot Card Combination?

A tarot card combination is created whenever two or more cards appear together in a reading.

Each card keeps its own basic meaning, but the other cards can change how that meaning is expressed.

For example, The Sun beside the Three of Swords may suggest healing and clarity after emotional pain. The same Three of Swords beside The Tower may point towards a more sudden or difficult emotional shock.

📖

Avoid Reading Every Card in Isolation

Beginners often explain one card, stop, then move on to the next.

This can make a reading sound like several unrelated definitions rather than one useful message.

After reading each card, ask:

  • How does this card connect with the card beside it?
  • Do the cards support or challenge each other?
  • Is the situation improving, worsening or changing?
  • What overall story do the cards create?
1

Start With the Spread Positions

Before combining the cards, remember what each position means.

A card in a challenge position should not be read in exactly the same way as the same card in an advice position.

For example, The Hermit as a challenge may suggest isolation. As advice, it may encourage quiet reflection and independent thinking.

The positions give the cards their roles within the reading.

→

Read the Cards as a Story

Imagine that each card represents one part of a sentence or one scene in a story.

In a past, present and future spread, look for movement from the first card to the last.

Ask:

  • Where does the story begin?
  • What is happening now?
  • Where does the current direction lead?

This approach creates a more natural interpretation than treating every card separately.

⇄

Notice Whether Cards Support or Challenge Each Other

Some cards strengthen one another because they share similar themes.

The Chariot and Strength together may emphasise determination, confidence and self-control.

Other combinations create tension. The Four of Pentacles beside The Fool may show a conflict between holding on to security and taking a new risk.

Contradictory cards are not a mistake. They often reveal the central conflict within the situation.

♡

Look for Repeated Suits

A spread containing several cards from the same suit usually places extra emphasis on that area of life.

  • Cups often highlight emotions, relationships and intuition.
  • Pentacles often highlight work, money, health and practical matters.
  • Swords often highlight thoughts, communication, truth and conflict.
  • Wands often highlight motivation, creativity, action and ambition.

The repeated suit may reveal the main focus of the reading even before every card is interpreted.

7

Look for Repeated Numbers

Repeated numbers can create a pattern across different suits.

Several Aces may point towards new opportunities. Several Fives may suggest instability or adjustment. Several Tens may show completion or the end of a cycle.

You do not need to memorise complicated numerology. Simply noticing that a number repeats can add useful context.

☀

Notice Major Arcana Cards

Major Arcana cards often carry more weight than Minor Arcana cards.

If several Major Arcana cards appear together, the reading may concern an important period of change, personal development or a larger life lesson.

A spread filled mostly with Minor Arcana cards may relate more closely to everyday situations and practical choices.

👑

Notice Court Card Patterns

Several court cards may represent people, personalities or different ways of approaching a situation.

Pages may suggest learning, curiosity or early development. Knights often bring movement and action. Queens may represent understanding and internal mastery, while Kings may represent authority and outward control.

Consider whether the court cards appear cooperative, distant or in conflict with one another.

👁

Look at Where the Figures Are Facing

The direction of the people in the cards can create a visual conversation.

Two figures facing each other may suggest cooperation, attraction, communication or conflict.

A figure turning away from another card may suggest avoidance, separation or leaving something behind.

These visual details are especially useful when the traditional meanings seem unclear.

🎨

Compare Colours and Symbols

Repeated colours, objects, landscapes or symbols can link cards together.

You may notice water appearing across several cards, suggesting a strong emotional theme.

Repeated mountains may suggest challenges, persistence or a goal that requires effort.

Trust what stands out visually, especially when the same detail appears more than once.

⚖

Identify the Strongest Card

Some spreads contain one card that feels more important than the others.

It may be a Major Arcana card, a card in the central position or simply the image that draws your attention first.

Use that card as the central theme, then consider how the surrounding cards explain, support or challenge it.

✧

Example: Two-Card Combination

Imagine drawing The Fool followed by the Eight of Pentacles.

The Fool suggests a new beginning, curiosity or a leap into unfamiliar territory.

The Eight of Pentacles represents practice, commitment and learning through repeated effort.

Together, they may suggest beginning a new skill and accepting that confidence will grow through patient practice.

⚡

Example: Contrasting Cards

Imagine drawing the Four of Pentacles beside the Wheel of Fortune.

The Four of Pentacles may represent holding tightly to stability or resisting change.

The Wheel of Fortune suggests movement, cycles and changing circumstances.

Together, they may show difficulty accepting a change that cannot be fully controlled.

☾

Example: Three-Card Story

Imagine drawing the Five of Cups, The Star and the Six of Swords.

The Five of Cups may represent disappointment or emotional loss.

The Star introduces healing, hope and renewed trust.

The Six of Swords suggests gradually moving away from difficulty.

Together, the cards create a story of emotional pain followed by healing and a slow transition towards a calmer period.

?

Ask One Connecting Question

When you feel stuck, ask:

“What are these cards saying together that none of them could say alone?”

This shifts your attention away from memorised definitions and towards the message created by the complete spread.

⚠

Common Combination Mistakes

  • Reading every card as an unrelated definition.
  • Ignoring the positions of the spread.
  • Searching online for a fixed meaning for every possible pair.
  • Assuming contrasting cards make the reading incorrect.
  • Using too many cards before becoming comfortable with smaller spreads.
  • Drawing extra cards whenever the message feels unclear.
✓

A Simple Combination Method

  1. Read the question again.
  2. Identify the position of each card.
  3. Give each card one simple meaning.
  4. Notice repeated suits, numbers and symbols.
  5. Decide whether the cards support or challenge each other.
  6. Identify the strongest card or central theme.
  7. Join the cards into one or two clear sentences.
🌱

Practise With Small Spreads

Two-card and three-card spreads are ideal for practising combinations.

Try drawing two cards and completing this sentence:

“This situation involves ___, which leads to or is influenced by ___.”

The goal is not to find a perfect interpretation. The goal is to practise creating a clear connection.

Remember

Tarot combinations do not require memorising thousands of fixed pairings. Focus on the question, spread positions, repeated patterns and the story created by the cards together.

Practise connecting the cards in your next reading.
