Guides / Reversed Cards

Understanding Reversed Tarot Cards

One of the first questions many beginners ask is whether tarot cards should be read upside down. The answer is simple: you can use reversed cards if they help your reading, but you do not have to. Many experienced readers never use them, while others include them in every reading.

↕

What Is a Reversed Tarot Card?

A reversed tarot card is simply a card that appears upside down when it is drawn.

Some readers intentionally shuffle so cards can appear in either direction, while others always keep their deck facing the same way.

The card itself does not change. What changes is the way the reader chooses to interpret it.

✓

Do You Have to Use Reversed Cards?

No.

There is no universal rule that says reversed cards must be included in a tarot reading.

Many professional readers only read upright cards because they believe every card already contains both its strengths and its challenges.

Others enjoy the additional layer of meaning that reversals can provide.

🌙

Why Some Readers Avoid Reversals

Some readers prefer to keep things simple.

Every tarot card already contains a wide range of possible meanings depending on the question, neighbouring cards and spread position.

Adding reversals can sometimes make beginners feel overwhelmed by trying to memorise twice as many meanings.

⭐

Why Other Readers Love Reversals

Reversed cards can introduce extra detail and encourage a reader to consider the situation from another angle.

Rather than automatically meaning "bad", a reversal often suggests that something is blocked, delayed, hidden or developing internally.

For many readers, reversals add depth without replacing the original meaning of the card.

🔒

Blocked Energy

One common interpretation is that the energy of the card is present but not flowing freely.

For example, the Ace of Cups reversed could suggest that emotional openness is being held back rather than completely absent.

The opportunity still exists, but something may be preventing it from being fully expressed.

⏳

Delayed Energy

Some readers see reversed cards as representing events that have not yet fully developed.

Instead of saying "no", the card may simply suggest "not yet."

This interpretation works especially well when surrounding cards indicate progress that is happening more slowly than expected.

🪞

Internal Energy

Another popular approach is to see reversed cards as experiences taking place inside rather than outside.

A reversed Strength card may represent inner confidence that still needs to grow instead of visible courage shown to the world.

The energy exists but is experienced privately.

⇄

Does Reversed Mean the Opposite?

Not always.

This is one of the biggest beginner misconceptions.

A reversed card is rarely the complete opposite of its upright meaning.

Instead, think of it as a variation, a reduction, a blockage or a different expression of the same core energy.

🎓

Should Beginners Learn Reversals Immediately?

There is no rush.

Many beginners spend months learning upright meanings before introducing reversed cards.

Doing so helps build confidence without doubling the amount of information to remember.

🃏

Example: The Fool Reversed

The upright Fool often represents new beginnings, curiosity and taking a leap of faith.

When reversed, some readers may interpret the card as hesitation, fear of change or taking unnecessary risks without preparation.

Notice that the core idea remains the same—starting something new—but the energy is expressed differently.

⚡

Example: The Tower Reversed

The Tower upright often represents sudden change, disruption or a powerful realisation.

Reversed, it may suggest resisting necessary change, delaying an inevitable transformation or experiencing the upheaval internally rather than outwardly.

Again, this is not the opposite meaning. It is another way the same energy may appear.

🎉

Example: Three of Cups Reversed

The upright Three of Cups is commonly linked with friendship, celebration and community.

A reversed interpretation might point towards misunderstandings within a group, feeling excluded or struggling to reconnect with friends.

The theme of relationships remains, but the circumstances have changed.

❌

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Assuming every reversed card is negative.
  • Trying to memorise two completely separate meanings for every card.
  • Ignoring the question being asked.
  • Ignoring the surrounding cards.
  • Treating reversals as predictions of disaster.
  • Changing interpretation simply because a card is upside down.

Remember that context is always more important than orientation.

💡

A Simple Beginner Method

If you decide to use reversed cards, keep your approach straightforward.

  1. Read the upright meaning first.
  2. Ask yourself whether the energy feels blocked, delayed or internal.
  3. Consider the position of the card in the spread.
  4. Look at the surrounding cards.
  5. Choose the interpretation that best fits the whole reading.

There is no need to invent a completely different meaning every time a card appears reversed.

🌱

Can You Change Your Mind Later?

Absolutely.

Many readers begin by using only upright cards and introduce reversals months or even years later.

Others try reversals for a while before deciding that they prefer reading upright only.

Tarot is a skill that develops over time. Your reading style can develop too.

Remember

Reversed tarot cards are optional. They are simply another way of exploring the same card, not a sign that something bad is about to happen. Learn the upright meanings first, then introduce reversals if they feel helpful to your practice.
